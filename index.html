Domain

Our Research Domain

Federated learning is a cutting-edge technique in machine learning that allows models to be trained using data stored on multiple decentralized devices or servers, while ensuring the data remains on these devices. This approach has several advantages, including improved privacy protection and the ability to train models on data distributed across different locations. However, it also introduces specific challenges related to security and privacy that need to be addressed. One of the key aspects of federated learning is the detection of potential attacks. Detecting attacks is crucial to maintaining the integrity, security, and effectiveness of the collaborative model training process. This research paper focuses primarily on identifying and preventing specific types of attacks within the context of federated learning. These attacks include model poisoning, data poisoning, and the Sybil attack, each of which poses unique threats to the system. To achieve the goal of detecting these attacks, various advanced methodologies are employed. These techniques involve the use of mathematical tools such as min-max scaling, Grubbs testing, and the Cauchy mean value theorem. When an attack is successfully detected, it triggers an alert mechanism that notifies relevant parties of the security breach. Additionally, the findings and outcomes of the detection process are visualized and presented through a dedicated dashboard called 'FedSec.' In summary, this research paper delves into the critical area of safeguarding federated learning environments by detecting and mitigating specific types of attacks that could compromise the confidentiality and reliability of the model training process.

Detecting Data poisoning attack

we outline the approach utilized to improve the detection of data poisoning attacks, specifically focusing on label flipping attacks, within the context of Federated Learning (FL). This approach encompasses various stages, including data preprocessing, model training, server-side dataset comparison, and the implementation of security measures aimed at preserving the integrity of the FL framework. To enhance the quality of the dataset, several data preprocessing techniques were applied. These techniques involved procedures such as data augmentation and image resizing. Additionally, to ensure consistency across devices, we employed min-max scaling, which normalized pixel intensity values to fall within the [0, 1] range. In terms of model updates, all contributions from participating users were gathered centrally on the server while employing privacy-preserving aggregation methods. During the model aggregation phase on the server, a crucial step involved the comparison of labels assigned by different users to the same chest X-ray images. This cross-verification process was essential. To determine the label most frequently assigned to each data sample, we employed a majority voting mechanism. Instances where data samples exhibited inconsistent labels across participating devices were flagged as potential indicators of label flipping attacks. Furthermore, we implemented an alert mechanism designed to notify us of any detected anomalies in the system.

Objectives of the research component:

  • Define Input Boundaries for Data Validation
  • Apply Min-Max Scaling for Consistent Data Ranges
  • Implement Server-Side Label Comparison for Data Integrity
  • Identify and Display Label Switches or Invalid Ranges

Detecting model poisoning attack

Detecting model poisoning attacks in federated learning is a critical concern, as they can compromise the integrity and performance of the collaborative machine learning process. In this scenario, federated learning comprises a server, often referred to as the global model, and a set of client nodes, each representing a local model. These client nodes are numerous (denoted as 'n') and utilize their respective datasets to generate predictions in multiple training iterations. The objective is to ascertain if any model poisoning attacks have occurred during this iterative process. During each iteration, the F1 score is computed for the predictions made by the local models. The F1 score is a widely used metric in classification tasks, quantifying the balance between precision and recall. It serves as an indicator of the model's performance, with a higher F1 score indicating better predictive accuracy. For instance, in Iteration 1, the F1 score might be calculated as 0.8, and in Iteration 2, it could be 0.9. This process continues for multiple iterations. To identify potential anomalies indicative of model poisoning, the F1 scores from each iteration are subjected to an outlier detection algorithm known as the Generalized Extreme Studentized Deviate (ESD) test. The Generalized ESD test is a statistical technique designed to detect outliers in a dataset, which in this context, would signify unusual or malicious behavior in the F1 scores. By applying the Generalized ESD test to the F1 scores across iterations, it becomes possible to uncover any patterns or instances of anomaly within the data. Upon detecting an anomaly pattern using the Generalized ESD test, further investigation is carried out to determine when the model poisoning might have commenced. This information is crucial for understanding the extent of the attack and taking appropriate countermeasures. Once the initiation point of the poisoning is identified through the ESD testing, a signal is relayed to a centralized dashboard or monitoring system. This real-time alerting mechanism ensures timely intervention and mitigation of the model poisoning attack, thereby safeguarding the integrity of the federated learning process. the approach outlined in this academic context involves a systematic process of monitoring F1 scores across iterations, utilizing statistical techniques such as the Generalized ESD test to identify anomalies, and signaling anomalies to a dashboard for prompt action. This methodology provides a proactive means of detecting and responding to model poisoning attacks in federated learning settings, thereby enhancing the security and reliability of the collaborative machine learning framework.


Objectives of the research component:

  • Calculate F1 score
  • Calculate generalized ESD test
  • Compare them

Detecting Sybil attack

The approach detailed here represents an advanced strategy used to uncover Sybil attacks within the confines of a federated learning ecosystem. The Sybil attack detection methodology commences by computing what is known as the "average model distance." This average model distance assumes a pivotal role in the identification of potential Sybil clients. The process entails several pivotal stages: Initially, the methodology computes the distance sum, representing the aggregation of model parameters derived from bona fide clients. These genuine clients are expected to contribute legitimate and accurate model updates throughout the federated learning progression. The resultant sum, designated as the "distance sum," serves as a benchmark for appraising the conduct of other clients. For each client partaking in the federated learning process, the methodology calculates the minimum distance between their model parameters and the reference distance sum. Essentially, this step gauges the degree of alignment between a client's model and the amassed model parameters of legitimate participants. The pivotal criterion for the identification of Sybil clients involves contrasting the computed minimum distance for each client with the distance sum. Should the minimum distance for a specific client exceed the distance sum, it raises suspicion of a potential Sybil attack. In simpler terms, clients whose model parameters significantly deviate from the established norm of legitimate participants are red flagged as questionable. This condition is systematically evaluated for all participating clients, enabling a comprehensive appraisal of each client's behavior. The methodology meticulously verifies whether each client's minimum distance surpasses the distance sum. Clients meeting the criteria where their minimum distance exceeds the distance sum are pinpointed as Sybil clients.

Objectives of the research component:

  • Launching sybil attack
  • Calculating model distance
  • Compare model distance
  • Identify sybil attack
